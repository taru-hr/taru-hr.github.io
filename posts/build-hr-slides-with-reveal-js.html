Turn your slides into a website — a beginner's guide to Reveal.js

You already know how to make slides. This is the same skill, saved as a web page you can share with a link, present in a browser, and update in seconds. No coding background needed — if you can edit a sentence, you can edit a slide.

By Taru Parttimaa · HR Specialist

I took one of our own HR decks — an Expat Employee Guide for people relocating to Finland — and rebuilt it as an interactive web presentation. Same content, but now it opens in any browser, works on a phone, and lives on this site at a shareable link. The tool that does it is called Reveal.js, and it's free.

Here is the finished result, running live. Click it and press the → arrow (or swipe on a phone):

The live deck · open it full-screen ↗

By the end of this guide you'll understand every piece of how that was made, and you'll have a copy-paste starter you can reuse for your next onboarding deck, policy briefing or training session. You never have to memorise anything — I'll translate each bit into plain English.

1. What Reveal.js actually is

Think of it like this. PowerPoint is a program you open. Reveal.js is a small helper that turns a plain text file into a slideshow inside a web browser. You write your slides as text; Reveal.js handles the arrows, the transitions, the full-screen mode, the speaker notes and the progress bar.

📊

PowerPoint keeps your slides inside a .pptx file that needs PowerPoint (or similar) to open.

🌐

Reveal.js keeps your slides inside an .html file that opens in any browser — no software to install, nothing for your audience to download.

Why bother, as someone in HR moving into analytics? A few honest reasons:

  • Share with a link. Send a URL instead of a 30 MB attachment. It's always the latest version.
  • Lives with your work. A web deck sits right next to your dashboards and reports — same site, same look.
  • It's just text. That means you can copy, find-and-replace, and reuse slides effortlessly — the analyst's mindset.
  • It's free and open. No licence, and it works offline once loaded.

In plain English

A Reveal.js presentation is one web page. Each slide is a chunk of that page. You edit the words; Reveal.js makes it behave like a slideshow.

2. One file, three simple parts

Open the demo file (posts/expat-guide-finland-slides.html) and, past the comments at the top, it's just three parts. You only ever touch the middle one. 

<!-- PART 1: the setup — load Reveal.js and the fonts (leave this alone) -->
<link rel="stylesheet" href="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/reveal.js@5.2.1/dist/reveal.css">

<!-- PART 2: your slides — this is the part you edit -->
<div class="reveal">
  <div class="slides">
    <section>Slide one</section>
    <section>Slide two</section>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- PART 3: the engine — start the show (leave this alone) -->
<script src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/reveal.js@5.2.1/dist/reveal.js"></script>
<script> Reveal.initialize(); </script>

That's the whole idea. cdn.jsdelivr.net is just a place on the internet that hands your browser a copy of Reveal.js — that's what "CDN" means, a delivery service for code. Because you're borrowing it from there, there is nothing to install.

🥪

A helpful picture. Your slides are the filling; Part 1 and Part 3 are the bread. You write the filling and leave the bread as-is.

3. Writing a slide

Every slide is one <section>. The word "section" is just HTML's name for "a block." Whatever you put between <section> and </section> becomes one slide. Here's a real one from the deck: 

<section>
  <h2>Your employment contract</h2>
  <p>Before you sign, make sure you understand:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Job title & main duties</li>
    <li>Salary, pay date & benefits</li>
    <li>Probation and notice periods</li>
  </ul>
</section>

Four tags do almost everything you'll ever need. Tags come in pairs — one to open, one with a / to close:

  • <h2>…</h2> — a heading (the slide title).
  • <p>…</p> — a paragraph of text.
  • <ul>…</ul> — an unordered list (bullets).
  • <li>…</li> — one list item (a single bullet).

Want a numbered list instead of bullets? Swap <ul> for <ol> (o for "ordered"). That's the level of difficulty we're talking about.

Try this

Copy that <section> block, paste it directly below itself, and change the words. You just added a slide. Reorder slides by moving whole <section> blocks up or down — cut and paste, exactly like dragging slides in the sidebar.

4. Revealing one point at a time

In PowerPoint you use "animations" to make bullets appear on click. In Reveal.js you add the word fragment to anything you want to appear one step later: 

<ul>
  <li>Prepare your documents</li>
  <li class="fragment">Visit a Finnish bank</li>
  <li class="fragment">Send your IBAN to payroll</li>
</ul>

The first bullet shows immediately; each class="fragment" bullet waits for another press of the arrow. class="…" is simply how you pin a label onto something so Reveal.js knows to treat it specially. Great for walking a new hire through a checklist without overwhelming them.

5. Stacking related slides (the down arrow)

Reveal.js has a neat trick PowerPoint doesn't: slides can stack vertically. The audience moves right through your main story, and down into optional detail. In the demo, the "Tax card" slide has a detailed comparison table stacked underneath it.

You do it by putting sections inside a section: 

<section>
  <section>Tax card — the basics</section>   <!-- press ↓ for more -->
  <section>The different tax-card types</section>
</section>

The outer <section> is now a "stack," and each inner one is a slide you reach with the down arrow. Perfect for "here's the summary — press down if you want the deep dive," so your main flow stays short.

6. Pictures and full-screen backgrounds

To drop an image onto a slide, use one <img> tag pointing at a file in your assets/images folder: 

<img src="../assets/images/taru-hr-logo.png" alt="Taru HR logo">

To fill the whole slide with a photo — like the Helsinki harbour on the title slide — you don't use <img> at all. You add an instruction to the <section> itself: 

<section data-background-image="../assets/images/helsinki-harbour.jpg"
         data-background-opacity="0.42">
  <h1>Expat Employee Guide</h1>
</section>

data-background-image is the "wallpaper for this slide," and data-background-opacity="0.42" dims it to 42% so your text stays readable on top. The alt="…" on a normal image, by the way, is the description a screen-reader announces — always worth filling in.

7. Speaker notes only you can see

This is the feature that sells people on Reveal.js. You can attach private notes to any slide and see them — with a timer and a preview of the next slide — on your own screen, while the audience sees only the slide. 

<section>
  <h2>Salary & payslip</h2>
  <aside class="notes">
    Remember to explain gross vs net — it's the thing new
    arrivals ask about most.
  </aside>
</section>

Anything inside <aside class="notes"> never appears on the slide. To read your notes while presenting, press the S key — a second "speaker view" window opens. (Try it on the demo above.)

8. Tables, without any special tool

The tax-card comparison in the demo is a plain HTML table. Structured comparisons — leave types, benefit tiers, before/after — almost always read better as a table than as bullets. The pattern is: a row (<tr>) holds cells (<td>), and header cells use <th>: 

<table>
  <tr><th>Type</th><th>Who it's for</th></tr>
  <tr><td>Standard</td><td>Staying over 6 months</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Source tax</td><td>Staying up to 6 months</td></tr>
</table>

The demo's theme already styles tables to match the gold-and-dark look, so you just write the rows and it looks the part.

9. Making it match your brand

The colours, fonts and spacing all live in one <style> block near the top of the file, under a section labelled THE THEME. At the very top of it is a short list of colours with friendly names: 

:root {
  --bg:   hsl(0, 0%, 7%);      /* page background (near-black) */
  --text: hsl(0, 0%, 90%);     /* normal text (light grey) */
  --gold: hsl(45, 100%, 68%);  /* the accent colour */
}

Change --gold to a different colour and every gold accent in the whole deck updates at once — titles, bullets, the progress bar. That's the same "one place to change it" idea behind a good spreadsheet. You don't need to understand the rest of the style block to recolour a deck.

Colours in plain English

hsl(45, 100%, 68%) reads as hue 45 (a golden-yellow), 100% colourful, 68% bright. Nudge the first number to shift the colour, the last to make it lighter or darker. Or just paste a hex code like #e0b23a — both work.

10. Presenting it

Open the page in a browser and use these keys. (They work on the live demo above — click it first, then try them.)

  • → Next slide  ·  Space does the same
  • ← Previous slide
  • ↓ Go down into a stacked detail slide (then ↑ to come back)
  • F Full screen
  • S Speaker view — your notes, a timer and the next slide
  • O Overview — every slide as a grid, click to jump (Esc also works)
  • B Blank the screen to black (for a pause / discussion)
  • ? Show the full list of shortcuts

On a phone or tablet, swipe left/right and up/down. It's the same deck — it just reflows to fit the screen.

11. Publishing it (you already have)

Because a Reveal.js deck is an ordinary web page, putting it on this site is the same as adding any other page:

  1. Save your .html file in the posts/ folder.
  2. Add a card for it in data/posts.json so it shows up on the Blog — copy an existing entry and point its "link" at your file (there's a walkthrough in templates/post.template.html).
  3. Commit and push. GitHub Pages publishes it at a public link you can share.

That's exactly how the Expat Employee Guide above got onto the Blog page — it's a normal post whose link happens to point at a slideshow.

Previewing before you publish

A single Reveal.js file opens fine by double-clicking it. If you want to preview it inside the full site (with the blog card), run the little local server from the project folder — python3 -m http.server 8000 — then open http://localhost:8000. On the live GitHub Pages site it just works.

12. A starter you can copy

Here is a complete, working presentation — small enough to read in one go. Save it as posts/my-first-deck.html, open it, and start replacing words. It already shows the pieces from this guide: a title background, bullets, a fragment, a vertical stack and a speaker note. 

<!DOCTYPE html>
<html lang="en">
<head>
  <meta charset="UTF-8">
  <title>My first deck</title>
  <link rel="stylesheet" href="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/reveal.js@5.2.1/dist/reveal.css">
  <link rel="stylesheet" href="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/reveal.js@5.2.1/dist/theme/black.css">
</head>
<body>
  <div class="reveal">
    <div class="slides">

      <!-- Title slide -->
      <section>
        <h1>Onboarding</h1>
        <p>Your first week, one slide at a time.</p>
      </section>

      <!-- A slide with bullets, one revealed on click -->
      <section>
        <h2>Day one checklist</h2>
        <ul>
          <li>Collect your ID badge</li>
          <li>Meet your team</li>
          <li class="fragment">Set up payroll with HR</li>
        </ul>
        <aside class="notes">Press S to see me — I'm a speaker note.</aside>
      </section>

      <!-- A stack: press the down arrow for detail -->
      <section>
        <section><h2>Benefits</h2><p>Press ↓ for the detail.</p></section>
        <section><h2>Annual leave</h2><p>Accrued monthly.</p></section>
      </section>

    </div>
  </div>

  <script src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/reveal.js@5.2.1/dist/reveal.js"></script>
  <script src="https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/reveal.js@5.2.1/plugin/notes/notes.js"></script>
  <script>
    Reveal.initialize({ hash: true, plugins: [ RevealNotes ] });
  </script>
</body>
</html>

This version loads Reveal's ready-made "black" theme (the second <link>), so it looks smart with zero styling effort. When you want the gold-and-dark house style instead, copy the <style> block from the Expat deck and remove that theme line. There are other built-in themes too — white, league, moon, sky — just change the word in the URL.

Your five-minute first win

Save the block above as posts/my-first-deck.html, double-click to open it, and press →. Then change "Onboarding" to your own title and refresh. That's the entire loop: edit words → save → refresh. Everything else is just more of the same.

So… is that really all?

Really. Slides are <section> blocks; headings, paragraphs and bullets are a handful of tags; fragment reveals things on click; stacked sections give you optional detail; and a note tucked in <aside class="notes"> is yours alone. The look is one list of colours you can change. That's a complete, professional presentation.

You don't need to remember the code — keep the demo file and this page as your cheat sheet. The valuable thing is the mental model: a deck is a web page, each slide is a block of it, and Reveal.js makes it move. Once that clicks, you can turn any of your HR material — onboarding, policy updates, analytics readouts — into something you share with a link.
