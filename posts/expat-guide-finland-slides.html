Step 01

Visa & residence permit

Before relocating, make sure you can live and work in Finland legally.

Who needs a permit?

EU / EEA & Swiss citizens
No visa needed. Register the right to reside for stays over 90 days.

Non-EU / EEA citizens
A residence permit for employment is generally required before arriving.

Application process

  1. Receive your employment contract
  2. Apply for the right residence permit
  3. Verify your identity at an embassy / service point
  4. Wait for the decision
  5. Travel to Finland after approval

Step 02

Your employment contract

Before you sign, make sure you understand each of these:

  • Job title & duties — role, reporting line, main tasks
  • Employment type — permanent / fixed-term, full / part-time
  • Working hours — weekly hours, flexibility, overtime
  • Salary & benefits — pay, pay date, bonuses, benefits
  • Probation period — length and terms
  • Annual leave — entitlement and how it accrues
  • Notice period — for both you and the employer
  • Collective agreement (TES) — may set minimum terms

💡 HR Tip

If anything is unclear, ask your HR representative before signing — and always keep a signed copy for your records.

The TES (Työehtosopimus) point matters for internationals — a collective agreement can guarantee minimums the individual contract doesn't spell out.

Step 03

Personal identity code (henkilötunnus / "hetu")

Why you need one

  • Banking
  • Healthcare
  • Payroll
  • Housing & contracts

How to get it

Register with the Digital and Population Data Services Agency (DVV). HR can guide you through the appointment and papers.

⏳ If it's delayed

HR can arrange temporary workarounds so payroll and access aren't blocked.

The hetu is the master key to Finnish services. Almost everything downstream — bank, tax card, Kela — needs it, so start it as early as possible.

Step 05

Opening a bank account

Why it matters

  • Salary — employers pay into a Finnish account
  • Digital ID — bank credentials log you into services
  • Daily life — bills, online banking, payments

🗓️ Timeline

From same-day to 1–2 weeks, depending on the bank and document checks.

  1. Prepare documents
    Passport/ID, residence permit, hetu, employment contract
  2. Visit a bank
    Nordea · OP Financial Group · Danske Bank
  3. Send details to HR
    Give your IBAN to payroll before the deadline
The chicken-and-egg problem: you often need the hetu for the bank, and the bank for salary. Flag this early so nobody is surprised when the first payday needs an IBAN.

Step 06

Tax card — "Verokortti"

Your tax card sets how much income tax is withheld from your salary.

What it is

Issued by the Finnish Tax Administration. It holds your personal tax rate, based on your estimated annual income.

To apply, you need

  • Valid ID & hetu
  • Estimated annual salary
  • Employment details

📤 HR & Payroll

Submit your tax card before your first payday. Payroll uses it to withhold the correct amount.

⚠️ If it's missing

Payroll may have to withhold tax at a much higher rate until a valid card arrives.

Step 07

Salary & payslip

💶 Pay day

Usually once a month on the agreed date — e.g. the 15th or the last banking day.

Your pay may include

  • Base salary
  • Overtime (if any)
  • Bonuses / allowances
  • Taxable benefits

Deductions

  • Income tax
  • Pension contribution
  • Unemployment insurance
  • Other statutory items

🧾 Read your payslip

Gross = before deductions · Net = what lands in your account. Spot something odd? Tell Payroll/HR promptly.

💡 HR Tip

Keep your tax card and bank details up to date to avoid payment delays.

The gross-vs-net gap surprises internationals the most. Walking through one real payslip in the first month prevents a lot of worried emails.

Step 08

Working hours

37.5
standard hours / week
9–15
core hours — be reachable for meetings
  • ~7.5 hours per day, Monday–Friday
  • Flexible start / end times by team agreement
  • Manage your time around deadlines & coordination
  • Hybrid / remote options may apply, with manager approval

Exact terms follow company policy and Finnish labour-market practice.

"Core hours" is the concept to land: outside 9–15 people genuinely flex. It's often a culture shift for new arrivals from longer-hours workplaces.

Step 09

Social security

How it works

Finland's system is residence-based and administered mainly by Kela. Coverage usually depends on residing — or working under certain conditions — in Finland.

🌍 Cross-border agreements

Finland has social-security agreements with certain countries. On an international assignment these can change where contributions are paid and which country's rules apply.

Contact HR before relocating if you're unsure.

Totalisation agreements are genuinely complex; the honest message is "ask HR early," because the answer depends on the person's home country and assignment type.

Step 10

Annual leave

  • Accrual — earned per month worked; amount depends on service & the collective agreement
  • Holiday year — typically 1 April → 31 March
  • Requesting — via the HR system / your manager, agreed in advance
  • Carry-over — unused leave may carry over or be compensated per policy

Example entitlement

24
summer holiday days
(May–Sept)
6
winter holiday days
(Oct–April)

💡 HR Tip

Check your contract, TES and company policy for your exact entitlement.

The 24 + 6 split (summer vs winter) is a Finnish specialty. Worth naming explicitly — new hires often assume they can take all leave whenever they like.

Step 11

Occupational healthcare

Provided by the company in line with Finnish labour regulations.

Services typically include

  • General medical consultations
  • Preventive healthcare
  • Mental-health support
  • Physiotherapy (as applicable)

You'll get detailed instructions on how to access these services when you start.

🚑 Emergencies

For medical emergencies, always use national emergency channels — call 112.

Distinguish occupational healthcare (day-to-day, work-related, non-urgent) from emergencies (112). People mix these up under stress.
